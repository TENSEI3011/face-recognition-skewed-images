Introduction

Face recognition from Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) footage presents a unique convergence of classical computer vision challenges and modern deep learning capabilities. Unlike traditional face recognition in controlled laboratory settings, UAV-based surveillance systems must contend with unconstrained altitude variation, significant pose skew, motion blur, H.264 video compression artefacts, and extreme resolution degradation — all occurring simultaneously in real deployments.

This literature survey reviews the foundational and state-of-the-art research across ten key domains that directly underpin the design decisions made in this project. Each section explains what was found in the literature and how that finding shaped a specific component of the implemented system.

Scope This survey covers literature from 1991 (Eigenfaces) to 2021 (SCRFD, Real-ESRGAN), reflecting both the historical foundations and the current state-of-the-art methods directly implemented in this system.

Face Detection — From Classical to SCRFD

2.1 Classical Methods

Viola and Jones (2001) introduced the first real-time face detector using Haar-like features with a cascade classifier, achieving rapid detection on CPUs but failing under significant pose variation or low resolution [1]. Dalal and Triggs (2005) proposed the Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG) feature combined with a linear SVM, demonstrating robustness to moderate illumination changes [2].

2.2 Deep Learning Detectors

Zhang et al. (2016) introduced the Multi-task Cascaded Convolutional Network (MTCNN), simultaneously predicting bounding boxes and five facial landmark points in a three-stage cascade [3]. MTCNN remains widely deployed but struggles with faces smaller than 30 pixels — a critical limitation for UAV footage. RetinaFace (Deng et al., 2020) advanced the field by jointly predicting face boxes, landmarks, and 3D face mesh vertices, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on the WiderFace benchmark across all difficulty levels [4].

2.3 SCRFD — Sample and Computation Redistribution

Guo et al. (2021) proposed SCRFD, a principled strategy for allocating computational budget across detection scales using Neural Architecture Search. The det_10g.onnx model from InsightFace's buffalo_l pack achieves near-RetinaFace accuracy at significantly reduced inference cost (10 GFlops vs 37 GFlops for RetinaFace) [5].

Key Finding Applied in this Project SCRFD uses an internal 640×640 detection grid. For a 1920×1080 frame, the internal scale factor is 0.33×, meaning a 30-pixel face becomes only 10 pixels on the grid — below the reliable detection threshold. This finding motivated passing full-resolution frames to the detector, with max_side=0 (no downscaling) in the video processing pipeline.

Handcrafted Feature Extraction

3.1 HOG — Histogram of Oriented Gradients

Dalal and Triggs (2005) demonstrated that local gradient direction histograms computed over dense overlapping cells capture shape information robustly across illumination changes [2]. Applied to aligned 112×112 face crops, HOG captures edge structure around the eyes, nose bridge, and mouth region — features that remain stable even under moderate compression.

3.2 LBP — Local Binary Patterns

Ahonen et al. (2006) applied Local Binary Patterns to face recognition, achieving real-time performance competitive with much heavier methods [6]. Uniform LBP encodes local texture by comparing each pixel to its 8 circular neighbours, producing a rotation-invariant binary code. LBP is particularly valuable for UAV imagery because it is illumination-invariant (relative thresholding) and degrades gracefully with H.264 compression.

3.3 Geometric Landmark Features

Kazemi and Sullivan (2014) proposed an ensemble of regression trees for real-time detection of 68 facial landmarks [7]. Normalised inter-landmark distances and angles form a compact geometric descriptor that is scale-invariant and rotation-invariant, capturing structural identity information that is complementary to appearance features.

Multi-Modal Motivation The survey established that no single feature type is sufficient for degraded UAV conditions. HOG, LBP, Geometry, and ArcFace embeddings each capture different aspects of identity — their combination through PCA dimensionality reduction and SVM classification provides robustness across the full degradation spectrum.

Deep Learning for Face Recognition

4.1 DeepFace and FaceNet

Taigman et al. (2014) demonstrated that deep CNNs trained on large labelled datasets could achieve near-human face verification accuracy (97.35% on LFW) [8]. Schroff et al. (2015) introduced FaceNet, using a triplet loss to map face images to a compact 128-D Euclidean embedding space where L2 distance correlates directly with identity similarity [9]. FaceNet established the embedding-space paradigm that ArcFace builds upon.

4.2 The Hypersphere Embedding Paradigm

A sequence of papers improved upon softmax classification by reformulating the decision boundary in angular/cosine space. SphereFace (Liu et al., 2017) introduced a multiplicative angular margin [10], CosFace (Wang et al., 2018) applied a fixed cosine margin [11], and ArcFace (Deng et al., 2019) introduced an additive angular margin on the geodesic distance on a unit hypersphere [12]. ArcFace provides the clearest geometric interpretation and strongest discriminability among these methods.

ArcFace and Metric Learning

Deng et al. (2019) trained ArcFace on MS-Celeb-1M (5.8 million images, 85,000 identities), achieving 99.83% accuracy on LFW, 98.02% on MegaFace, and 94.20% on the challenging IJB-C benchmark [12]. The ResNet-50 backbone produces 512-dimensional L2-normalised embeddings where cosine similarity between same-person pairs is consistently ≥ 0.6, and between different-person pairs is typically ≤ 0.3.

Critical Finding for UAV Application The survey revealed that ArcFace was trained on studio-quality images where faces occupy the full image frame. When applied to UAV footage where faces are 17–30 pixels wide, the cosine similarity between a probe face and its gallery counterpart drops to 0.25–0.45 due to the information loss from the small face size. This motivated lowering the FAISS threshold from 0.62 to 0.35 for the video processing pipeline.

Open-Set Recognition and FAISS Matching

7.1 The Closed-Set Problem

Bendale and Boult (2015) identified a fundamental limitation of all softmax and SVM classifiers: they operate in closed-set mode, always assigning a query to one of the known training classes even when the true identity is not enrolled [21]. In a surveillance scenario, this produces false identifications for every unknown person passing through the camera's field of view — unacceptable for any real deployment.

7.2 Open-Set Recognition via Threshold Rejection

The literature review revealed that cosine similarity thresholding over ArcFace embeddings provides a principled open-set gate: if the best cosine similarity between a probe embedding and all gallery embeddings falls below a threshold, the probe is classified as UNKNOWN rather than being forced into a known class. This maintains ArcFace's discriminative power while adding open-set capability.

7.3 FAISS for Scalable Similarity Search

Johnson, Douze, and Jégou (2019) introduced FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search), a library for efficient exact and approximate nearest-neighbour search over high-dimensional vector spaces [22]. The IndexFlatIP (inner product) index over L2-normalised vectors computes cosine similarity and returns the nearest gallery embedding in under 1ms for galleries up to 100,000 entries on CPU — enabling real-time open-set face recognition.

Image Quality Enhancement and Super-Resolution

Wang et al. (2021) demonstrated that Real-ESRGAN, a generative super-resolution model trained on synthetically degraded images, can recover perceptually sharp texture from 4× downscaled face images and improve recognition rates by 5–15% on standard benchmarks [23]. For the practical video processing case, learned SR models add 50–200ms of inference latency per frame — prohibitive for real-time use.

Practical Solution Adopted The survey revealed that bicubic upsampling with INTER_CUBIC interpolation, when applied to the padded face crop region before the alignment warp, provides meaningful improvement over bicubic upsampling after alignment. This is because the alignment warp itself introduces resampling artefacts that compound with the initial interpolation. The adopted approach crops the face region with 30% context padding, upscales to 112px using INTER_CUBIC, then performs the similarity transform alignment — matching the input format ArcFace expects.

Temporal Consistency in Video

Single-frame face recognition in video is unreliable because any individual frame may be affected by motion blur, partial occlusion, unfavourable lighting, or compression artefacts at the exact moment of capture. Wu and Ji (2017) reviewed temporal smoothing approaches in video face recognition, finding that majority voting over a rolling window of 10–20 frames reduces false positive rate by 60–80% with negligible computational overhead [25].

The temporal voter implemented in this project uses a 10-frame rolling window requiring 5-frame majority (50% consensus) before committing to an identity. This means the system absorbs up to 5 incorrect or ambiguous single-frame predictions before changing the displayed identity — eliminating visual flickering and improving user trust in the displayed result.

Evaluation Methodology

10.1 CMC Curves — Rank-k Identification Rate

Phillips et al. (2010) standardised the Cumulative Match Characteristic (CMC) curve as the primary evaluation metric for closed-set face identification in surveillance scenarios [26]. The CMC plots Rank-k identification rate (the probability that the correct identity appears within the top-k returned matches) as a function of k. Rank-1 is the most stringent metric; Rank-5 is the practical operational metric for a human operator reviewing a shortlist.

10.2 ROC Curves, EER, and TAR@FAR

The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve plots True Accept Rate (TAR) vs. False Accept Rate (FAR) at varying thresholds. The Equal Error Rate (EER) — the operating point where FAR = False Reject Rate — provides a single scalar for threshold-independent comparison. TAR at FAR = 0.1% is the standard operational metric for surveillance: it measures how many enrolled persons are correctly identified while accepting fewer than 1 impostor per 1,000 queries.

10.3 d-prime Discriminability Index

Signal detection theory's d' index (Green and Swets, 1966) measures the separation between genuine-pair and impostor-pair score distributions in units of standard deviation [27]. d' > 2.0 indicates strong separability between enrolled and non-enrolled individuals; d' < 1.0 indicates the system cannot reliably distinguish them.

Key Findings Summary

Finding 1

Standard detectors fail on UAV-altitude faces without full-resolution input

SCRFD's internal 640×640 detection grid causes a 30-pixel face in a 1920×1080 frame to appear as only 10 pixels on the grid when the frame is naively downscaled. Full-resolution input is required for reliable detection of small UAV faces.

Finding 2

No single feature type is sufficient — multi-modal fusion is required

HOG captures shape, LBP captures texture, geometry captures structure, and ArcFace captures deep identity. Each degrades differently under UAV conditions. Their combination through PCA + SVM provides complementary robustness that no single feature achieves individually.

Finding 3

SVM and softmax classifiers cannot handle unknown individuals

All closed-set classifiers will always identify a probe as one of the enrolled classes, generating false positives for unknown individuals. FAISS cosine similarity thresholding provides principled open-set rejection, returning "UNKNOWN" when the best gallery similarity is below the threshold.

Finding 4

UAV pose and altitude cause 15–40% accuracy degradation

The literature establishes that recognition accuracy drops non-linearly with yaw angle (beyond ±30°) and face resolution (below 64 pixels). UAV footage routinely presents both challenges simultaneously — the pose study experiment directly quantifies this effect in the project's specific data distribution.

Finding 5

Single-frame video predictions require temporal smoothing

Rolling-window majority voting over 10 frames reduces false positive rate by 60–80% with no additional model cost. This is essential for producing stable, trustworthy identity labels in a live surveillance video feed.
